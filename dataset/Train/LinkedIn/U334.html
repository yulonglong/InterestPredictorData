        Background
        Summary
        Shortly after graduating high school in 1985, I started a career in Land Surveying and attended the Southern California Joint Surveyors Apprenticeship Program, a highly-regarded training mechanism for the surveying profession that spans 4 years. I climbed the surveying ladder and became the party chief of a 3-person survey crew in less than two years.
        

        
Prior to graduation from high school, I had hacked my Atari 400 gaming console so I could write my own programs. This led to great interest in computers and technology in general. Starting in 1988 and concurrent with my Land Surveying employment, I repaired computer hardware and software for local clients. By 1991, I had constructed my own bulletin board system (BBS) for locals to connect and discuss various topics.
        

        
By 1993, my Land Surveying career had advanced to an office position where I prepared data for field crews, reduced field notes, performed COGO functions and prepared survey maps in CAD. These computerized tasks were a natural fit for my desire to use computer technology.
        

        
In 1998, I earned registration as an Oregon Professional Land Surveyor.
        

        
In 1999, my self-taught web programming skills had reached a level where I could start a company building websites.
        

        
In 2001, achieved registration as an Arizona Registered Land Surveyor.
        

        
In 2003, I partnered with two other people to build an online discussion forum for Land Surveyors that became very popular. Due to problems with one of the partners, myself and the other partner decided to shut down the website permanently about a year later.
        

        
By 2005, I had co-managed the Land Surveying Department at a large company in Tucson. During my time there, I also developed CAD Standards specific to our department and helped train coworkers via written tutorials, workshops and personal instruction.
        

        
In 2006, I went full-time offering web development and hosting services via Harness Technology.
        

        
In 2010, I founded SurveyorConnect, a discussion forum for Land Surveyors.

        Project Surveyor
        G. E. Raleigh and Associates
        January 1998 – August 2001 (3 years 8 months)
        I provided project coordination, COGO and CAD for a variety of right-of-way projects for natural gas, high-voltage overhead power lines, telecommunications and other municipal utilities. The process included initial and continual contact with client, field location surveys, processing of data, preparation of electronic drawings, coordination with public and private utilities and delivery of final product to the client.         Project Surveyor
        i.e. Engineering
        January 1993 – January 1998 (5 years 1 month)
        For the first 6 months, I was a party chief working on construction projects, land title surveys, timber boundary surveys and other types of surveys. Then I became a survey technician, where I learned how to process field data, compute boundaries (including COGO routines) create survey maps (subdivisions, boundary surveys, etc.) and write legal descriptions. By 1998, I had obtained my Professional Land Surveyor's license. I had also become very proficient with AutoCad and had become a point of reference for those who needed help.
        Crew Chief
        HR Engineering
        September 1989 – January 1993 (3 years 5 months)
        I started out running my own 3-person field crew, using most of the principles I learned from my previous employer. After the first year, I started working on non-construction type projects, such as topographies, ALTA/ACSM Land Title Surveys, boundary surveys and more. The most notable project was a land title survey of an animal shelter for the City of Corona. The City was so pleased with the outcome that they personally visited the office to thank me and provide an award of merit for my work.
        Crew Chief
        Jim Boyd Surveying
        January 1986 – September 1989 (3 years 9 months)
        I started as the "grunt" on a 3-person survey crew, working mostly on large subdivision projects and master-planned communities in the Riverside, CA area. I quickly moved up in rank and by the time I reached my two-year anniversary, I was running my own crew. Most of that experience was based in construction and often troubleshooting and correcting engineering plans. At that time, it was still common to provide construction staking using a Wild T-16 transit, a chain and an HP-41cx to calculate stake locations by hand. We often used Super Surveyor software hardwired into an HP-71b for roadway calculations and to set centerline control for curb and utility stakes. The T-16 was used to set the offset stakes. Elevations were placed on the hubs using a standard level and Philly rod. It was not uncommon for us to brag about setting (with tacks) and elevating 2,000 feet of curb stakes in a single day.
